DEMAND

  • Demand is the qty of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at a time and price
  • A demand schedule shows individual demand - a chart showing qty demanded and prices
  • As price increases, demand decreases
  • Demand curves {DC’s} have a negative derivative
  • External Non Price determinants of demand:
    • Income
    • Tastes and Prefs
    • Num Buyers
    • Demographic Change
    • Govt Policy
    • Season
  • Qd = quantity demanded
  • a = autonomous demand [if price = 0, Q intersect]
  • b = change in quantity due to price change
  • P = Price

Changes in b shift the steepness of the curve, and represent how responsive consumers are to changes. Lower b = less sensitivity.

Supply

  • Supply is the qty of a good or service that producers are willing and able to produce at a time and price
  • A supply schedule shows individual demand - a chart showing qty demanded and prices
  • As price increases, supply increases
  • Supply Curves {SC’s} have a negative derivative
  • External Non Price determinants of supply:
    • Costs of production
    • Productivity {QtyOut/QtyIn}
    • Govinter {Tax/Regulations}
    • Price of Related goods
    • Supply Shocks

  • Qs = quantity supplied
  • a = autonomous supply [if price = 0, Q intersect]
  • b = change in quantity due to price change
  • P = Price

Market Supply and Demand are combined individual curves, the total quantity from the entire market at a given price and time.

Market Equilibrium and Efficiency

  • Equilibrium {EQL} is the state at which all contrasting forces cancel each other out
  • The equilibrium price {Ep} is where qty supplied and qty demanded are equal. It is also called the market clearing price.
  • Supply excess forces producers to lower P until surplus = 0
  • Demand excess forces producers to raise P until shortage = 0
  • Prices inform the producer What is to be produced
  • Optimization of prices inform How should it be produced

Solving For Equilibria:

  1. Solve For EP {equal to P at Qs = Qd}
  2. Sub into either equation to get EQ

Market Efficiency

  • Consumer Surplus {CS} is the benefit received by consumers when they pay a price below that which they are willing
  • Producer Surplus {PS} is the benefit received by producers when they are paid more than they would be willing to sell for
  • Marginal benefit {MB} is the benefit derived from each additional unit consumed
  • Marginal cost {MC} is due to the increasing cost of producing more units
  • Allocative Efficiency {AloEf} is achieved when production is such that MB = MC

Elasticities

  • Elasticity is responsiveness of consumers or producers to a change in a variable in the market
  • ELASTIC: Very Sensitive: Horizontal
  • INELASTIC: Insensitive: Vertical
  • Applications:
    • Firms will decide on pricing based on consumer sensitivity
    • Governments will consider elasticities so as not to destroy a market, and to optimize tax income. Taxing sensitive goods is a bad idea.

Types Of Elasticities

PED = Price Elasticity of Demand -> How much quantity demanded changes based on price

  • Interpretation
    • PED < 1 then there is inelastic demand, the curve is more vertical
    • PED > 1 then there is elastic demand, the curve is flatter
    • PED = 1, it is unit elastic demand. {20% incr in price = 20% less sales}
    • PED = 0, then any change in price is not important to the consumer. demand won’t change. ex O2 in a hermetically sealed room.
    • PED = ∞, then any change in price will cause an infinite change in qty demanded (shoelaces or other commodities)
  • Factors [SPLAT]
    • S - Number of substitutes - More substitutes = More sensitivity
    • P - Proportion of the consumer’s income the price of a good is - Higher Proportion = More sensitivity
    • L - Luxury - More Luxurious = More Sensitivity
    • A - Addictivity - Higher addictivity = Lower Sensitivity
    • T - Time that consumers get to respond is a major factor. * As time passes, PED becomes more elastic, though it is inelastic at first.* - More Time = More Sensitivity
  • Total Revenue Test:
    • if price is +ve proportional to total revenue, demand is inelastic. {You can increase prices}
    • if price is -ve proportional to total revenue, demand is elastic {You should decrease prices}
  • Revenue is a quadratic!

XED = Cross Price Elasticity of Demand -> How much demand for one product changes based on demand for another

  • Complementary goods have a negative XED - as the use of one requires the other.
  • Substitute goods have a positive XED
  • Interpretation
    • <1 XED means they are cross price elastic, consumers are sensitive to the price of another good (many substitutes, for example)
    • >1 XED means they are cross price inelastic, fewer consumers will migrate if prices go up on a good
    • XED = 1 means a direct correlation between the two goods
    • XED = 0 means the goods are unrelated
    • XED is how responsive consumers of good A are to a change in the price of good B. It can be pos or neg.
    • The size of the XED tells how closely related the two goods are.

PES = Price Elasticity of Supply– How much suppliers should change their supply qty based on changes in price of another product

  • Perfectly inelastic goods are usually fixed in their physical scarcity, like gold, or unique paintings by the dead.
  • Perfectly elastic goods are those inventoried in large quantities.
  • Factors
    • Time:
      • S1 = Market period, instant price change = Extreme inelasticity
      • S2 = Short term, a few months
      • S3 = Long term, elastic
    • Mobility of the factors of production
    • The ability to store stocks
    • The amount of unused capacity
      • Excess capacity is how much output an industry can produce in S2 beyond current level without requiring expansion. This means price changes can be quickly taken advantage of.
      • Full capacity means more factories and equipment must be used, which takes time and money.

YED = Income Elasticity of Demand -> How much quantity demanded for a good changes based on the income of a consumer

  • Normal goods have pos YED, inferior have neg YED
  • Applications - production based on future income possibilities, governments will not increase income tax in the goal of reducing spending if they know that it is elastic, as there will be little change.

Government intervention

Taxes and Subsidies

  • A Tax is charge placed on an indiv or firm payable to a govt under punishment of law
  • An Indirect Tax is a tax collected from supplier after supplier collects tax from the consumer
  • Specific Tax - A flat rate ($2/carton)
  • Ad Valorem Tax - A percentage on sales (13% HST)
  • Excise Tax: Placed on a specific good
  • Effect on Supply Curve
    • Specific Tax: A per unit tax shifts the SC back (upwards and left) by the amount of of the tax. This value is constant per unit.
    • Ad Valorem Tax: With the higher price of a good, the overall tax will increase so supply shifts back, but the distance between the init supply and post-tax supply increases. It is more inelastic.
  • Stakeholder Consequences from taxes:
    • Prices go up
    • Output goes down
    • Market size shrinks
    • Consumers suffer
    • Producers suffer
    • Governments benefit
  • Deadweight loss is the loss to output, a societal loss on both CS and PS
  • Tax Incidence is a measure of the consequence of a tax on all the affected parties
    • How much will the market shrink bc of the tax?
    • How badly will firms and their employees suffer?
    • Will the tax raise government revenue significantly or slightly?
    • IF PED ~ PES, the tax burden is shared equally
    • IF PED > PES, producers pay more, lower govt rev. is made, DW loss increases
    • IF PED < PES, consumers pay more, more govt rev., DW loss is less
  • A Subsidy is a payment from the govt to an indiv or firm to the purpose of increasing the purchase or supply of a good
  • Motivations: To increase consumption, support an industry, or make a good more competitive on the world market
  • These shift supply curves by the amount of the subsidy

  • Effects:
    • Shortages
    • Rationing

  • Effects:
    • Surplus
    • Reduced market size
    • Overproduction
    • Allocative inefficiency

    • Reparation: Excess could be thrown out, put into foreign markets, or the surplus may be purchased directly by the government

Common Access to Resources and Threat to Sustainability

  • The Tragedy of the Commons is where common goods that everyone has access to are often abused. Incentives are not towards the long term good, and the resources are exploited. Ex. Overfishing, Deforestation

Free Rider Problem - Some people benefit from a resource but do not pay for it - Waterways, the Thames river for example, are repositories of untold quantities of toxic waste.

  • Potential Solutions to Sustainability Problems
    • The lack of a price mechanism causes depletion of goods, property rights bind the goods to the consumer and they are invested in its long term survival
    • Carbon taxes decrease the consumption or production of carbon emitting products to where MSC = MSB
    • Tradable permits allow for taxing pollution levels where licenses for polluting are distributed to the public. Permit Quantity does not increase, so price for a permit due to increased demand increases considerably.

Perfect Competition

  • A perfectly competitive model assumes: 1 Identical products, goods that are not differentiable and perfect substitutes 2 Ease of entry and exit to the market 3 Price Takers 4 Large numbers of firms
    • Ex. Agriculture, Low skilled labour, low tech manufacturing
  • Revenue depends on 2 factors:
    • The saleable price of output
    • The quantity of output

  • Since they are price takers, the price is determined by market supply and market demand.

  • In the short run, a profit maximizing firm will produce where its MC = MR.

  • If at the quantity MC = MR, the ATC is less than its AR, economic profits equal to the Q x difference will be made.

  • If at QE, the ATC > AR, then loss minimization will take place. Losses are inevitable. If at QE ATC = D = AR, the company will earn a normal profit, and break even.

  • The market is in long run equilibrium when firms break even.

  • The market is in short run equilibrium when MC = MR.

  • Entry eliminates profits - Profits attract competitors who drive down the price until all firms are breaking even.

  • ** Exit eliminates losses -** Losses repel firms, who increase scarcity by their absence thus increasing prices.

  • Shut down occurs when P < AVC; or where TL > TFC #### Efficiencies

Formal Collusion: Cartel Formation

  • A cartel is an agreement whether formal or not, between competitive parties to limit competition and fix Prices
    • A cartel occurs when oligopolists agree to take specific market action in a coordinated and sustained effort to enhance profits, in continuous efforts in several forms:
      • Restricting Output
      • Raising prices
      • Restricting R and D and Innovation
      • Restricting Advertisements
  • This may be illegal, backstabbing is incentivized, competition may be drawn in
  • Informal collusion is when a firm establishes price leadership - it can survive a price war
  • Non Collusive oligopolies occur when firms do not collude:
    • Firms constantly face the choice to collude or compete, as described in the prisoner’s dilemma.
    • If a firm increases prices from the equilibrium, it will face more elastic demand because other firms won’t follow, and its market share will decrease.
    • If a firm lowers prices from the equilibrium, in order to steal market share, competitors will lower their prices, causing a price war which lowers TR for all firms.
    • There is thus an incentive to freeze prices.
  • Non price competition:
    • Based on Service/Demand/Appearance/Brand Power/Quality
    • Or reduction of costs #### Advantages and Disadvantages

Disadvantages

  • Allocative efficiency and productive efficiency cannot be achieved
  • There are incentives to take advantage of the consumer, to discourage competition, and to collude

Advantages

  • Continuous economic profits could exist due to the barriers of energy
  • Research and development could exist
  • Firms that innovate may make some economies of scale, which could ultimately lower prices for consumers.
