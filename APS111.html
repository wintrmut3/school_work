Design Process Overview

  • The design process is iterative
  • This stops before the ideal solution is met, but usually is not bad
  • The formal engineering design process (EDP) relies on tools and methods to craft: requirements and complete documentation
  • Creativity methods are used, to generate imaginative solutions

Project Phases

  • Design problems are open ended problems
  • A team must develop a problem statement which defines and guides the process
  • Once a problem is defined, ideas are generated, known as conceptual design (alternatives).
  • Evaluating these ideas requires decision making tools, comparing against the criteria and requirements to make sure they match
  • Reality checks are important, to check feasibility
  • Steps are:
    1. Design Brief
    2. Requirements
    3. Problem Statement
    4. Solution Generation
    5. Evaluation and testing

Navigating the Engineering Design Process

  • The design process is about defining and reshaping the design space - the set of possible solutions considered during a project.
  • Requirement generation defines the design space
  • New solution generation expands/populates this space, while discarding ideas shrinks it. Testing explores it.

Requirements Generation

  • Begin with a clear idea of what is being solved
  • This is defined by a problem statement and requirements (describing the functions that a solution must have to be valid).
  • The requirements also include objectives, constraints and context in which the solution will operate

Multi Use Design tools

  • These are techniques used in the design process to generate requirements, ideas, and solutions.
  • Idea generation tools can be used to make requirements or solution ideas

Decision Making

  • These tools reduce the design space, and are crucial to show what parts to pursue and discard

Iteration

  • Generate - Select - Reflect cycles allow you to move towards an optimal solution (not an ideal solution).

Investigating

  • Developing models and prototyping to test early design solutions to give an understanding of the problem and the solutions

Post Conceptual Design

  • This covers some common activities needed to implement a design

The Problem Statement

  • A client statement/design brief is used, and from it are extracted the project’s goals.
  • Sometimes, a solution-driven statement is provided with an implied solution, so an engineer must create a solution independent statement that only contains essential information and addresses the underlying need
  • Design Briefs may be problematic:
    • Implied solutions
    • Assumptions
    • Conflicting information
    • Typos

The Requirements

  • The EDP is essentially an information process
  • Requirements can be broken down into functions objectives and constraints
  • They require context, research, thinking and the client statement to produce

Teamwork

  • A team and a group are different because a team has a common purpose and uses the skills of all the members to solve a problem

  • Cross functional teams are interdisciplinary, their opposite is within functional teams

  • Teams need not have specific expertise, only to work together as a single entity

  • Pseudoteams work and create output less than the sum of its parts

  • Potential teams work at or slightly better than the sum of their parts

  • Real teams communicate effectively, going well beyond the capability of its individual parts

  • There are 5 common factors that make teams successful:

    1. Common goal
    2. Individual and group accountability
    3. Real work to be done, that is valuable and relevant
    4. Conflict and difficulty management strategies
    5. Group processing occurs
      [ People Issues, tasks, purpose ]
    • Reflections and lessons learned activites are crucial

Team Management

  • Team Leader ~ project manager
  • Oversees assignment of tasks, keep track of where the team is, deal with people issues, communicate with instructor
  • DO NOT MICROMANAGE
  • Create an Agenda and Meeting Minutes!
  • Regular team meetings shouldn’t be more than an hour or two
  • Minutes from last meeting (deadlines, tasks, decisions) become agenda for current meeting
  • Task timing is difficult, requiring >=1.5x of original time estimate
  • PERT/GANTT charts are used to record tasks and timings (but we need to know the tasks)

People Issues:

  1. Hitchhikers - Slackers
  2. Hijackers - “If you want something done right, do it yourself”, overly controlling and untrusting (don’t make them team leader)
  3. Isolationists - Not wanting to interact, non-communicating
  4. Enablers - Help everyone else, but get overwhelmed and cannot ultimately deliver

Critical Thinking in Design Documents

  • Complicated means having many interdependent parts
  • Complex means non deterministic
  • Project requirements are about looking at a project from multiple perspectives
  • CDS is about generating solutions and justify a design decision

Some Questions:

  1. Are the needs and wants described completely?
  2. What is the gap?
  3. What other stakeholder interests/perspectives exist?
  4. What is the context the solution will operate in?
  5. What are the related rules and regulations
  6. What needs to exist to give the client what they want?
  7. How can I maximize positive social and environmental impact?
  • Many solutions may be generated, and we must examine the unique trade offs and unique information/value provided
  • Costs/Environment/Social Impacts
  • Look at lessons learned documents, to see how well we did and what we could do better

Making and Supporting Statements

The 3 categories of statement are

  1. Opinion (evaluation made by experts or by feeling/judgement, requiring support)
  2. Facts - Statements of truth, supported by laws of nature or credible info sources (history)
  3. Claims are based on scientific evidence. Engineers use facts to support their claims/complete statements

Make a complete claim by stating your idea, explaining it and supporting it with evidence [ STATEMENT / EXPLANATION / EVIDENCE ]

Evidence - is scientific method/principle, research and your own data
Use value free language (not value laden) when making a statement

Characteristics of Good Requirements

The team must:

  • Agree on the problem being solved, including requirements
  • Agree on the scope
  • Prelim eval. on the scope of the project to determine solution set

Scope defines the breadth and depth of the problem, known also as limits or boundaries.

  • Scope creep can occur when projects just increase in scope indefinitely and stagnate
  • DFX considerations means DESIGN FOR X (X is a design consideration, like human factors, safety, a e s t h e t i c, manufacturability etc, …)
  • Add DFX to a list of Objectives (eg. design for safety for highways), and go back to them while constructing requirements

Characteristics of Good Requirements

  1. Completeness (Diminishing returns from going too far, 80/20 rule)
  2. Solution Independence - Only make forced decisions
  3. Testability/ Is there a metric?

Evaluating the Problem

Before trying to solve the problem, ask:

  1. Is there an algorithmic/accepted way of solving the problem? (Routine Design)
  2. Is there an off the shelf solution
  3. Is the project feasible?
  4. What parts of the problem are open/closed (Subproblem analysis)

Constraints ** NOT IN TEXTBOOK OR READINGS

  • We’re defining the boundary conditions of the design space
    • Objectives let us evaluate potential solutions
    • Constraints tell us what are outside the design space
    • You should try to minimize the constraints to allow as open a design space as you can while maintaining standards of safety and legality and such.

Objective Goal - If a design fails to meet an objective goal, it is less desirable
Constraint limit - If a design fails to meet the CL, it is rejected/deleted

Constraint Sources - Client, Regulations/Codes/Standards, Stakeholders - Indicated by must or shall

Regulations, Codes and Standards:

  • Regulations are laws like OSHA
  • Codes are interpretations of regulations in engineering-speech, a step by step list (eg. Fire codes)
  • Standards are not legally required but are what we choose to adhere to - like MILSTD or IEEE standard, or adhering to PIPA. They are mutually agreed upon eg. Connectors = USB
  • Designing to a standard acts as a shorthand, and international translation
  • TechStreet gives a source for standards

Common Pitfalls with Restraints:

  • Unmeasurables
  • Making up metrics or limits that come from nowhere
  • Confusing Obj and Constraints (These can match)
  • A project can be about a constraint and not a function, but dont mix them up

Functions:

  • What the design must do - Active statements, necessary for design solution
  • Primary functions are large and can be broken down into subfunctions, requiring careful analysis
  • Subfunctions enable the primary function
  • Functional basis is a technique that allows you to describe functions in very basic design terms. it defines a design from a few basic operations on a few flows
  • The black box method looks at energy/info/matter I/O and looks at conservational data, to figure out what the black-box design actually does

Functional Basis:

  • Operations on flows [Mass/Energy/Information]
  • Operations are:
    1. Sense
    2. Support (Prevent change)
    3. Combine
    4. Separate
    5. Replicate (Info only)
    6. Store
    7. Extract
    8. Transform
    9. Generate (Info only)
    10. Move through space/time
    11. Control - do all of those things above

Black Box Technique

  • Describes Mass, Energy and Information without describing the design itself
  • Shows only inputs and outputs, asks you to connect them

Decomposition

  • Breaking up a problem into parts, while generating requirements
    • Structural decomposition involves breaking into discrete units
    • Functional decomposition involves breaking functions into subfunctions and describing the parts needed to fulfil those functions at the end

Means Analysis

  • Decompose the problem into subfunctions, and generate as many ideas to fulfil one function, and look for commonalities - Functions including all the generated solutions probably are important functions (if every solution fits into the function “sticks air to dirt” then it’s probably an important function)

Benchmarking

  • A survey of existing designs, to analyze that which already remains in the market
  • Performance analysis involves identification of the subfunctions of the item and metrics usable to measure the performance (eg. volume of coffee over time = flow rate) (how long to roll up cord)
  • Task analysis involves looking at a view independent of the operator, looking at how many steps and what steps are needed to perform an operation, and how much detail is required (self observation) - used for idea gen, obj/reqs

Form/Flow/Function Analysis

Involves looking at the functions and how they’re enabled by the form of the design and the flows of mass/energy/info
Form includes size/shape/structure, material properties etc
Type and quantity of flows that are related to the subsystem

Why Benchmark?

To uncover new requirements, inspiration, preventing duplication
But it can anchor solutions and pollutes the design space

Objectives

  • Describes what the design solution should be
  • Measures of effectiveness, to evaluate solutions
  • Use an objective tree, with a list of objectives from client statement and DFX considerations, then decomposed to specific, measurable objectives
  • Eg. How Why Trees
  • Some objectives are mutually exculsive
  • Pairwise Comparison may be useful, comparing only one item at a time at the same point to create a prioritization and sort
  • Break down abstract objectives into measureable subobjectives

Pairwise Comparison

  • A way to prioritize a list, to eliminate ideas or items from csonideration
  • Only creates relative priorities and as an approximation
  • Tabulate in a matrix and decide which is more important (1/0), then order by total points

Using metrics

A metric is a method to measure a property of some tech
Comparison requires some parameter of a metric to differentiate solutions
When comparing, use approximates and check confidence levels - if it’s 1.1 and 1.0, and the error is ±0.1\pm 0.1±0.1 then they;re about equivalent. A 10% difference is probably the same as equivalency.
You can test by:

  1. Designing binary choices eg. must be a colour
  2. Estimation
  3. Analytical model
  4. Prototyping

Creativity Methods

Idea Generation

This is a divergent process to expand the design space
Use creativity methods to expose many solutions and evaluate from there, don’t just go with the first thing.

Brainstorming

  • Generates high quantity, criticismless, can be combined, gets all ideas out
  • Free Brainstorming is for idea generation, one recorder takes down all ideas for 15-20 minutes, eliminate duplicates and number ideas through multi voting
    • This may be chaotic, can cause conflict, and disadvantage the quiet
  • Structured brainstorming takes individual brainstorming and takes the best ideas, from each individual
    • It’s good for new teams, and but makes it difficult to build on others’ ideas. Requires multiple rounds

The key to creativity is MISTAKES!

Lateral Thinking is about coming up with other directions to think

  • Why/Why/Why (5)
  • Assumption Challenging
  • Reversal method
  • Random stimulus
  • Arbitrary Constraint
  • Blue sky thinking (no constraints)

SCAMPER allows you to branch off an idea:

Magic Solutions require nonsensical creations, utilizing “Magic” as a stepping stone to bridge the gap to a solution which may or may not be possible
